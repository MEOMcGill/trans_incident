# Key Takeaways

**1. Trans panic narratives were a significant but minority part of Tumbler Ridge shooting discussions, driven primarily by influencers.** Transgenderism-related content increased substantially after the RCMP confirmed the shooter was a trans woman, but represented a minority of posts about the shooting. Influencers were the main source of these discussions, while news outlets, politicians, and government organizations overwhelmingly focused on other aspects of the event.

**2. Negative rhetoric about trans people dominated engagement, especially violence association and identity denial.** Posts that attack, delegitimize, or marginalize trans people received a disproportionate share of engagement. Posts associating trans people with violence or denying transgender identity (e.g., misgendering the shooter) received by far the most engagement.

**3. Canadians are deeply polarized on gender identity issues along ideological lines, not along social media use.** Only 8% of left-leaning Canadians believe schools are indoctrinating children with gender ideology, compared to 66% of right-leaning Canadians. Age and gender play a more modest role. Social media use does not appear, in and of itself, to be related to these beliefs.

**4. We do not find evidence that transgender and non-binary Canadians are less likely to participate in online political spaces than other Canadians.** After controlling for age, political interest, social media use, education, region, and survey wave, transgender and non-binary Canadians report feeling more comfortable expressing their political views online and do so more frequently. These differences do not extend to offline speech, consistent with an activated minority framework.
